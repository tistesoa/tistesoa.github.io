Docfis

I take great pride in my involvement in this project, as it allowed me to apply the wealth of knowledge I've accumulated over the past six years. Serving as the technical lead, I successfully orchestrated the development of a Brazilian fiscal note issuer using a comprehensive AWS approach. The project was a significant undertaking, requiring the integration of various technologies and methodologies, and I am pleased to have led my team to achieve impressive results. In spearheading the project, my responsibilities included designing the end-to-end system, employing a full-stack AWS approach, and implementing cutting-edge technologies. This encompassed the use of microservices, saga pattern, event-driven architecture, and a single-page application for the web solution. The challenge we tackled was substantial â developing a Software as a Service (SaaS) platform designed to expedite the issuance of fiscal notes. Our solution successfully handled over 500,000 requests per day from more than 12,000 machine clients across Brazil. Notably, it operated seamlessly in offline mode, enhancing project resilience and reliability. We managed to deploy the solution into a production environment within a year, addressing clients across all states in Brazil, each with its own specific rules and requirements. Our cloud technology stack featured Java with the Spring Web Flux framework for reactive microservices, Node.js for serverless functions, SQS for messaging, and DynamoDB as the database solution. For offline functionality, we utilized Java with Quarkus and SQLite to create a lightweight embedded service. On the front end, Angular 12 with Material was employed. The entire infrastructure was orchestrated using Terraform, a tool I provided. During the course of the project, we encountered challenges, particularly with DynamoDB. In cases of high demand, the database was losing records due to insufficient configuration. To address this issue, I modified the offline submission processes to run at distinct periods, preventing spikes in data submission. Additionally, we allocated more resources to the database, enhancing its resilience and ability to handle increased data loads. As the head of the team, I maintained an inclusive and collaborative approach. All decisions and steps were shared with the team through meetings, presentations of Proof of Concepts (POCs), and discussions of design solutions. I actively participated in code reviews, provided guidance, clarified doubts, and resolved uncertainties related to our tasks. Finally, I played a pivotal role in maintaining reliability within the production environment. This involved addressing critical bugs promptly and scheduling new tasks for the team during noncritical scenarios.

Cards

In this project, I embraced the role of a Senior Developer, contributing remotely to a dynamic team engaged in building Microservices using Kotlin and frameworks such as Javalin and Spring. My entry marked the beginning of a new team focus: the creation and adaptation of the credit card service for legal entities. Initially, we adhered to the existing stack, leveraging the Kotlin language alongside the Javalin web framework to craft lightweight REST endpoints atop the Jetty server. We incorporated Koin for dependency injection, utilized the Fuel library for HTTP networking to facilitate communication between resources, and adopted a lightweight SQL library for seamless access to a PostgreSQL database. Beyond the familiar tech stack, the team also employed the saga-pattern to address significant challenges. It was my introduction to this concept, as well as my first experience with Amazon cloud services. I was impressed by how they effectively segmented business logic into steps, utilizing message queues for communication. This approach not only enhanced code maintainability but also fortified the project's resilience. As the months progressed, our team successfully integrated with another card team, streamlining our operations to have a single team responsible for all credit card demands. During this phase, we ventured into using the Spring framework for creating new services, a shift that, despite a slight increase in weight, significantly simplified code building. Notably, other teams adopted this approach as well. Our primary responsibilities involved creating new features and resolving bugs. One particularly interesting challenge involved communication with multiple services, necessitating the meticulous handling of failures and providing a tailored response for each connected server. To address this, we opted for an orchestration platform to manage diverse situations, be they successes or failures. Additionally, we prioritized minimizing data transfer between the Bank app and services, considering the limitations of bandwidth in mobile applications. Apigee served as our apigateway, consolidating all service calls and optimizing mobile backend interactions. Throughout this journey, I gained valuable insights into security programming practices, emphasizing the prevention of logging sensitive data, adhering to privacy protocols, and addressing unique considerations associated with remote work environments.

PPE

During my tenure as a Senior Developer in this on-site project, I had the opportunity to delve into microservices, REST, JSON, and Angular 2 with Bootstrap, particularly during the migration of an antiquated system built in Java2EE. This legacy system, which operated on a Weblogic Java platform and utilized Oracle as its database, was tasked with handling CRUD operations, protocols, petitions, and all lawsuit processes related to the state of Rio Grande do Sul in South Brazil. The challenges we faced with the old Java2EE system were numerous, ranging from memory leaks to the necessity of daily system restarts and the need to scale machines to accommodate the continuous influx of clients throughout the day. To address these issues, we opted for a solution involving the migration of all business logic to microservices built in Java 8 with CDI, while still maintaining the Weblogic platform. Simultaneously, we replaced the existing JSF frontend with Angular 2 and incorporated the Bootstrap interface library. As the migration initiative commenced, I took the lead in learning Angular and subsequently shared my newfound knowledge with my colleagues, ensuring a smooth transition for the team. Over the course of a year and a half, we dedicated ourselves to the comprehensive migration of the system. We achieved the milestone of running the system consistently without the need for daily restarts, leading to substantial improvements in response time. Beyond the immediate benefits, this migration not only modernized the system but also established a robust foundation for future enhancements and scalability. The project's successful evolution underlined our commitment to overcoming challenges, adopting contemporary technologies, and ensuring the sustainability of our systems.
